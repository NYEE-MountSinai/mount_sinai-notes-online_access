PROTOCOL SUMMARY

Overview

Objectives

To study ocular outflow dynamics (see appendix for definition and discussion of ocular outflow) of the eye using intraoperative tonographic biometry and assess its predictive value in improving postoperative outcomes

Endpoints

Measured post-operative IOP up to Day 90 and its correlation to intraoperative ocular outflow and IOP biometry in patients undergoing concomitant intraocular surgery

Rationale

Understanding intraoperative ocular outflow may have post-operative predictive management value to avoid immediate post-operative complications and improve outcomes

INTRODUCTION

Study Rationale

There are no current metrics to connect intraoperative findings to post-operative outcomes for intraocular surgeries that modify outflow. Abnormally high or low intraocular pressure (IOP) may occur after these surgeries and are due to an ocular outflow problem; yet, outflow is not directly measured at any point during a patient’s care. IOP is used as a proxy for ocular outflow, but is still not truly representative and is inadequate for outflow assessments. Many intraocular surgeries such as glaucoma, cataract, and retina surgeries modify ocular outflow but patients suffer from immediate post-operative complications from abnormal IOP because the outflow homeostasis of the eye is unknown.

Complications from abnormal IOP may result in permanent vision loss, suboptimal surgical outcomes, and unnecessary repeat surgeries. Therefore, we would like to measure the ocular outflow during surgery to better inform surgeons of post-operative management and outcomes.

Background

The main purpose of glaucoma surgery is to reduce the intraocular pressure (IOP). However, it is difficult to know how well the surgery will achieve this with current methods. As a result, patients may have high or low IOP in the post-operative period. In the case of trabeculectomy, for example, the rate of hypotony (too low IOP) has been reported to exceed 30% with a significant risk of follow-on complications. For glaucoma patients undergoing cataract surgery, the most common eye surgery worldwide, as many as 28% of cases with co-morbid glaucoma demonstrate abnormally high IOP over 30 mmHg and more than 50% experience IOP over 25 mmHg.

These abnormal IOP fluctuations place patients at risk for vision loss or suboptimal post-operative results. It has been studied that the utility of the standard day 1 post-operative visit after cataract surgery is indicated primarily to check for and treat high or low post-operative IOPs. Thus, having better control and prediction of the post-operative IOP could reduce the need for this potentially unnecessary office visit, which became especially critical during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Currently, there are no clinically useful bio-markers to predict the IOP post-operatively. While intraocular pressure measurement pre, peri and intra-operatively has been described, it is not efficient because of many confounding factors, and it does not closely correlate with the eye’s outflow facility. Simple intraoperative tonometry has not been useful either as it is not a sufficient surrogate for outflow capacity estimation, only predicting the immediate IOP, without relation to the post-operative steady-state outflow capacity. The most common devices used to measure IOP, tonometers, themselves typically also suffer from inaccuracy in their readings from a number of factors, such as corneal edema, which is common during eye surgery. Surprisingly, the current, most common standard method for assessing outflow intraoperatively is visual and tactile inspection by the surgeon. That is, the surgeon observes the amount of fluid leaking, palpates the eye, and estimates whether the amount is too much, too little, or acceptable. This makes it difficult to understand the outflow of intraocular fluid or pressure regulation in a dynamic system such as the eye.

Prior studies have assessed outflow capacity based on animal, cadaveric, or mathematical modeling. Few have performed an in-vivo evaluation of the IOP and outflow capacity. The team of Karyotakis et al (see attached reference) performed in-vivo measurements during routine cataract surgery in 19 patients. Patients had a baseline measurement taken upon entry into the eye, followed by an infusion of saline to pressurize the eye up to 40 mmHg, and then the IOP decay curve was graphed to calculate the ocular outflow.  This study was important in advancing the knowledge of ocular outflow, but it was limited in its scope in a variety of ways aside from the small sample size:
1.         It only assessed the outflow at the beginning of the surgical procedure. No measurements were taken during the cataract surgery and none were taken at the end.

2.         No correlation was made to post-operative outcomes. No post-operative data was collected or evaluated.

3.         Only healthy eyes undergoing cataract surgery were studied.  Glaucoma is a disease of ocular outflow and as such glaucoma surgery is entirely focused on modifying the outflow.  These patients, however, were excluded in addition to all other patients with other disease entities.

4.         The data analysis was purely mathematical as it was published in 2015. Given recent advancements in Artificial Intelligence (AI), there may be patterns or findings that went unnoticed without the aid of such technology.

Therefore, we propose this study to measure the IOP fluid dynamics and outflow homeostasis in patients undergoing intraocular surgery to understand how post-operative surgical success can be optimized and complications prevented.

STUDY DESIGN

Hypothesis

Intraoperative tonography and fluid biometry to determine the ocular outflow can be predictive of post-operative outflow and abnormal IOP

Type of Trial

Prospective observational study, Single-Site

Methods

Recruit physicians affiliated with the Mount Sinai Health System who are performing intraocular surgeries, such as cataract and/or glaucoma surgeries. Enroll patients to undergo concomitant IOP measurement and outflow capacity calculation during their surgery. Monitor IOP post-operatively for up to 90 days. Single study arm

Measurement Details

Measurements will be collected and surgical management of the patients will not be influenced based on measurements as surgeons will be blinded to them during the surgery. Measurements will be taken using an FDA-registered, sterile, single-use, disposable Deltran I pressure sensor designed for measuring physiological pressures of the body. The sensor will be attached to an infusion line communicating to an infusion source at a point out of the sterile field. It outputs measurement data electronically to Microsoft Excel. Computations and analysis will be performed with standard software such as Microsoft Excel.

Methods To Minimize Risks

Risk will be low by design because measurement of IOP during surgery will occur through existing incisions created for the surgery itself.

Stratification

Patients will be stratified according to disease entity (glaucoma and/or cataract), severity (mild, moderate, etc), and surgery performed (cataract surgery, cataract surgery combined with glaucoma surgery, trabeculectomy, and/or glaucoma device implantation)

End of Study

Up to 150 people enrolled or a predictive model of post-operative IOP is established. Therefore, if a predictive model is determined prior to 150 people, the study will end.

Rationale For Study Design

Rationale for overall design

Surgery provides an opportunity to directly measure ocular outflow. Current methods are all indirect, such as measuring static IOP with a tonometer depressing the cornea.  Outflow modifying surgeries, such as cataract and glaucoma surgeries, also present opportunities to study how different interventions can modify the outflow.

Study design rationale

As only measurements will be taken, no "intervention" is being performed to affect the outcome of the surgery or patient's care.  The surgeon is masked to the findings during the surgery so will be unable to make intraoperative surgical management changes to care. Therefore this is observational. As this is observational, a control is not needed.  An observational design has been chosen to collect data to develop a predictive model of post-operative findings without influencing them.

While this is an observational study, that refers to the design.  The study itself sometimes involves pressurization of the eye that may occur to obtain some measurements, which is not a passive observational measurement.

Follow-up will be for 90 days as this will allow patients' IOP to stabilize after the acute post-operative period of 30 days.

Study Details

Setup

·       The sterile, single-use, disposable pressure sensor (Deltran I Disposable Pressure Transducer) will be attached to a stable location within the sterile field as close as possible to the subject’s eye without interfering with the surgeon’s access, such as underneath the eyelid speculum temporally near the subject’s temporal orbital rim aka the side of their head.

·       The sensor will connect fluidically to the anterior chamber using a standard off-the-shelf 20G Lewicky Anterior Chamber Maintainer that is already available at the hospital via a luer lock

·       The sensor will be fixated in the sterile field by the surgeon using tegaderms or other standard sterile fixation material

·       The sensor will communicate to the infusion source (such as a sterile bag of BSS hung on an IV pole) via tubing that will extend off the sterile field.

·       The surgeon will connect these components (sensor, AC maintainer, and IV tubing) via luer locks under the guidance of the study staff.  Surgeons have adequate training in making these connections as they regularly use luer lock connections as a part of standard care

·       Prior to insertion into the eye through a clear corneal paracentesis, the tubing will be primed to eliminate any air bubbles and ensure a continuous laminar column of fluid

Measurements

·       Measurements will be taken during the surgery through the existing paracentesis that is necessarily created to perform the surgery itself

·       The anterior chamber maintainer will be periodically inserted through the incision at the beginning, during, and end of the surgery, at the surgeon’s discretion

·       Other sources of infusion, such as a phacoemulsification handpiece, will be removed from the eye prior to taking measurements

·       Measurements will be recorded using the pressure sensor as it is connected in-line with infusion tubing

·       The pressure sensor is designed to measure the pressure in-line and be reflective of the pressure measured at the distal tip of the tubing. This is because the flow in the tubing is laminar and the height differential between the sensor and the point of the tip (the anterior chamber) will be accounted for through calibration.

·       Periodic measurements and infusions to characterize the outflow capacity (facility) will be performed at the surgeon’s discretion

·       The infusion will consist of raising the height of the pole with the infusion bag to an appropriate height to pressurize the eye to a desired value no greater than 40mmHg (as was done in the study by Karyotakis et al) or as dictated by the surgeon, such as in the case of a glaucoma patient. Once the pressure reaches the desired value, the infusion line will be closed and the decay of the eye pressure monitored. Measurements will be continuously taken before, during, and after the infusion. At any point, the surgeon can stop the measurements at their discretion. Should the IOP not decay and stay elevated, for example at 40 mmHg, the surgeon/study staff can stop pressurization and drain fluid from an existing incision to decrease the IOP to an appropriate value.

·       The sensor is designed to output measurements electronically

Conclusion

·       Surgeon will remove the AC maintainer from the eye at their discretion, at least at the conclusion of surgery

·       All equipment is single-use, sterile, and disposable, except for the AC maintainer that is reusable and designed to be resterilized.

·       Disposable equipment will be discarded according to the standard appropriate waste protocols

·       The AC maintainer will be sent for resterilization according to the hospital’s standard policy

·       Total time for measurements will take less than 10 minutes

·       Measurements that have been collected will be analyzed using standard software such as Microsoft Excel

Subject Details

Recruitment

o   Ophthalmic surgeons performing intraocular surgery within the Mount Sinai Health System will consent subjects and enroll patients prior to surgery in a preoperative visit. Subjects will be informed of the study and can sign the consent form or if they choose to can sign the consent form at a later date.

o   No screening is required as the surgeons themselves will be identifying subjects

o   Patients will be consented by the surgeon/surgeon’s staff in a language they understand

o   All surgeons will be added to the study team prior to participation

Inclusion Criteria

Individuals undergoing cataract and/or glaucoma surgery in the Mount Sinai Health System

Exclusion Criteria

Individuals less than 18 years old, pregnant women, those without capacity to make their own decisions, and prisoners

Participant Discontinuation/Withdrawal from the Study

Subjects may withdraw from the study at any time. After the surgery is complete, the patient will be followed by the surgeon for their post-operative care and their IOP measurements will be collected at each visit as a part of standard care. These measurements can be collected from the patient's medical record without interacting with the patient during the post-operative period. As this collection of data from the record will cause no modification to their standard care, it is less likely that patients will voluntarily withdraw from the study; nonetheless, should they choose to, they can withdraw at any time. Pre-operative measurements will be collected from chart review.

For patients who require further incisional surgeries due to complications from the first surgery, the measurements will stop.  If additional in-office procedures are required, then in-office measurements can continue according to the schedule.

If, during the surgery, a patient becomes unstable while measurements are being taken, measurements will be halted. The surgeon may restart measurements once the patient is stabilized or aborted completely per the surgeon’s discretion.

Lost to Follow-Up

Patients in the study will be participating in their surgeon’s regular post-operative care and will be required by the surgeon to follow-up, which will aid to minimize loss to follow-up. Patients who have not followed-up or miss a visit will be contacted by the surgeon’s office to remind them of follow-up.

SAFETY

Safety Monitoring

Periodically, such as after the first 10 cases, the data and cases will be reviewed to determine if a predictive algorithm can be identified and if any safety concerns are noted. Once a predictive algorithm is identified, the study is complete. If any safety concerns are noted at such a time point as well, the study may be modified or discontinued as necessary.

Surgeons will be the ultimate decision-makers regarding the safety of their patients, as they will know their patients’ medical conditions best. As such, the safety of patients included in the study will be monitored by their surgeons in concert with study staff.  As patients will be undergoing surgery as recommended by their surgeon to treat an existing condition, such as cataracts or glaucoma, the post-operative care and safety will be dictated by the surgeon.

Adverse events (AE) will be defined as those events that negatively affect the patient’s health specifically as a result of the study.  As there is no intervention (no additional incisions and no medication or surgical procedure performed), an AE must be clearly related to the study and not to the surgery itself.  Adverse events will be monitored and recorded as they relate to the patient’s outcome from the surgery. Surgeons will report to study staff any adverse events immediately as they are identified. The management will be according to the treating physician. 

Risks/Benefits

Known Potential Risks

The risks of standard intraocular surgery exist and are unavoidable for surgery.  Intraoperative tonometry will be performed through the incision created for the surgery.  There will be minimal additional risk, as such measurement is not a routine part of surgery but is unlikely to affect the surgical outcome.

Toxic Anterior Segment Syndrome (TASS) is known to occur unpredictably in routine, uncomplicated ophthalmic surgeries. Most commonly, it is due to improperly cleaned reusable instruments (Mamalis N). It has also been found to occur in cases due to BSS that was prepared in the hospital pharmacy (Andonegui). As TASS is a known risk in all eye surgery, the occurrence of TASS may not be associated with the study and may be coincidental. However, since reusable anterior chamber maintainers will be used and sterilized according to the hospital's standard protocol, their use could theoretically increase the risk of TASS.  Other equipment is sterile, single-use, and disposable. BSS will not be prepared in the hospital pharmacy and will only be used that is directly purchased from the manufacturers.

No published data exists on increased risks associated with increasing ophthalmic surgical time for less than 10 minutes (Cheng et al). Neither are there data on risks associated with artificially pressurizing the eye during surgery up to 40 mmHg as will be done in the study.  Should a surgeon feel such pressurization could cause harm, the pressurization can be modified to the surgeon’s choosing or aborted completely at any time.  For reference, phacoemulsification machines, such as the Alcon Centurion, that are commonly used at the hospital are set by default to pressurize the eye up to 70 mmHg.

Known Potential Benefits

A predictive model may be developed to inform surgeons of better surgical and post-operative management strategies, including potential pre-operative risk-stratification. This model may provide better outcomes for patients and reduce complications from surgeries in the future.

ANALYSIS

Statistical Analysis

Hypothesis

Intraoperative ocular outflow measurements using tonography are predictive of post-operative findings such as IOP

Null Hypothesis

Intraoperative dynamic tonometry measurements and their tracing are not predictive of postoperative IOP states and major IOP fluctuations (hypotony and hypertension episodes)

Primary Efficacy Endpoint(s)

A potentially predictive model is developed correlating the intraoperative IOP dynamic tonometric measurements and their tracing to the post-operative IOP state.

Secondary Efficacy Endpoint(s)

Incidence and rate of adverse events such as IOP spikes and hypotony as a byproduct of the surgery itself

Sample Size Determination

This is a preliminary feasibility and exploratory study which will use standard statistical analytical methods, followed by a machine learning model to correlate intraoperative biometry data to post-operative outcomes. It is expected to enroll up to 150 people to get a first-pass assessment of feasibility.

Populations for Analyses

All patients who meet inclusion/exclusion criteria will be included in the analysis. Analysis will be broken down further by type of surgery, such as cataract surgery alone vs glaucoma surgery alone vs combination cataract and glaucoma surgery etc.

Approach

IOP and outflow data will be collected for statistical analysis, followed by artificial intelligence machine learning analysis to identify patterns predictive of post-operative outcomes

Discontinuation

If a predictive model is identified prior to enrolling 150 people the study may be discontinued

DATA HANDLING

Confidentiality, Privacy, Storage, and Retention

·       Patient data will be anonymized and records kept at the surgeon's office in compliance with HIPAA

·       Data will be collected and stored on a on a Sinai-secured network location. .

·       Data will be kept for seven years

APPENDIX

Ocular Outflow Definition and Discussion

The outflow of the eye is the flow of aqueous humor through the natural pathways that eventually lead back to the body’s circulatory system. The aqueous humor is the fluid created by the eye itself to serve a variety of purposes ranging from nutritional support to providing an optically clear medium for light transmission to facilitate vision. 

When there is disharmony between the rate of aqueous humor production and the rate that the aqueous humor leaves the eye, there exists an abnormality in ocular outflow. For example, if the rate of aqueous humor leaving the eye is less than the rate of production, then the outflow is low.

One such manifestation of abnormal outflow is an abnormal IOP. In the example above, if the outflow of the eye is low and the rate of production is high, then the IOP would be high. The reverse would also be true.  In disease states such as glaucoma, people often have a low outflow and a high IOP.

Outflow and IOP are related but not equivalent, however. Outflow is a dynamic process; whereas, IOP is discussed in static terms. It is similar to the relationship between acceleration and the speed (technically velocity) of a car. For example, a car may have a high speed, but not be accelerating quickly if it is maintaining a constant speed. On the other hand, a car may be accelerating very quickly but the speed at that moment may be low.  Therefore, IOP provides only a limited snapshot of the eye’s ability to manage its aqueous humor flow.  As such we chose to examine ocular outflow in this study as it is a much better description of the eye’s aqueous humor flow regulation.

We will still utilize methods of determining the IOP, such as with a pressure sensor, to obtain ocular outflow.  For example, we will graph the IOP over time to generate an IOP curve. Then to determine the outflow, we will, in simple terms, take the derivative of the IOP curve.

Collecting intraoperative information on the outflow of the eye in this manner and correlating it to the post-operative measurements, using IOP at each visit as a proxy, will hopefully explain why some people develop post-operative complications related to an abnormal IOP (and outflow) and others do not.
